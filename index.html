About

I am a 2nd-year Ph.D. student in the Marine Geology & Geophysics (MG&G) program at the University of Washington School of Oceanography. My research focuses on seafloor pressure observations for vertical geodesy — developing and evaluating instruments that can detect millimeter-scale deformation of the ocean floor.

My current work centers on self-calibrating absolute pressure gauges (APGs) deployed at active sites including Axial Seamount and the Endeavour Segment, where I compare multiple instrument designs side-by-side to characterize sensor drift, viscoelastic behavior, and calibration performance over multi-year timescales.

Before joining UW, I completed my M.S. at Tokyo University of Marine Science and Technology under Prof. Daisuke Inazu, and worked as a civil engineer at Pacific Consultants applying marine science to coastal infrastructure adaptation for sea-level rise.

Research

🌊
Self-Calibrating Pressure Gauge Evaluation
In-situ side-by-side comparison of three APG designs (UW A-0-A, CSCPR, AZA) cabled within a 20 m radius at Axial Seamount Central Caldera (1,500 m depth). Multi-year analysis of drift reduction, spin-up behavior, and viscoelastic sensor characteristics using BOTPT as a stable reference.
🌋
Volcanic Seafloor Deformation
Monitoring magmatic inflation and eruptive deflation cycles at Axial Seamount using continuous cabled gauges and Mobile Pressure Recorder (MPR) surveys. Constraining magma chamber dynamics through Mogi-model inversions of multi-year pressure records on the Juan de Fuca Ridge.
📡
Slow Earthquake Detection
Applying calibrated pressure arrays at the Endeavour Segment and Hikurangi Trough to detect slow slip events (SSEs) and inter-seismic strain. Work at Endeavour has revealed previously unreported inflation in the aseismic southern margin, corroborated by hydrothermal temperature surges.
🔬
Ocean Bottom Pressure Modeling
Using numerical ocean models (HYCOM, GLORYS, ECCO2, SOM) to separate oceanic noise from seafloor deformation signals. Evaluated across six Pacific subduction zones — showing that atmospheric pressure loading (SOMp) provides 9–15% additional RMS reduction at western Pacific sites.
