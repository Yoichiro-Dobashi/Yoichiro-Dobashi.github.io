I study how the seafloor moves — one pascal at a time.

I'm a 2nd-year Ph.D. student in the Marine Geology & Geophysics program at the University of Washington School of Oceanography. My research focuses on seafloor pressure observations for vertical geodesy — developing and evaluating instruments that detect millimeter-scale deformation of the ocean floor.

My current work centers on self-calibrating absolute pressure gauges (APGs) deployed at active sites including Axial Seamount (Juan de Fuca Ridge) and the Endeavour Segment, where I compare multiple instrument designs side-by-side to characterize sensor drift, viscoelastic behavior, and calibration performance over multi-year timescales.

Before joining UW, I completed my M.S. at Tokyo University of Marine Science and Technology under Prof. Daisuke Inazu, and worked as a civil engineer at Pacific Consultants on coastal infrastructure and sea-level rise adaptation.

Selected Research

01
Self-Calibrating Pressure Gauge Evaluation
In-situ side-by-side comparison of three APG designs (UW A-0-A, CSCPR, AZA) cabled within a 20 m radius at Axial Seamount Central Caldera (1,500 m depth). Multi-year analysis of drift reduction, spin-up behavior, and viscoelastic sensor characteristics using BOTPT as a stable reference.

02
Volcanic Seafloor Deformation
Monitoring magmatic inflation and eruptive deflation cycles at Axial Seamount using continuous cabled gauges and Mobile Pressure Recorder (MPR) surveys. Constraining magma chamber dynamics through Mogi-model inversions of multi-year pressure records.
Juan de Fuca RidgeMPRMogi ModelVolcanism
03
Slow Earthquake Detection
Applying calibrated pressure arrays at the Endeavour Segment and Hikurangi Trough to detect slow slip events (SSEs) and inter-seismic strain. Work at Endeavour has revealed previously unreported inflation in the aseismic southern margin, corroborated by hydrothermal temperature surges.

04
Ocean Bottom Pressure Modeling
Using numerical ocean models (HYCOM, GLORYS, ECCO2, SOM) to separate oceanic noise from seafloor deformation signals across six Pacific subduction zones — showing atmospheric pressure loading provides 9–15% additional RMS reduction at western Pacific sites.
